I’ve never known what I wanted, but I’ve always known what I didn’t want. I didn’t want to be there. And I left as soon as I legally could. I graduated high school—my mom’s dream for all of her kids. She was just one year shy of attaining her idea of the highest standard of education. So she set her mind to making sure we achieved the supreme status of schooling. And in the south, in Arkansas, you graduated from more than just high school. Walking the stage meant you survived jail, drugs, and heaven. “I’m proud of my kids! They have never been in jail or done drugs.” Poor parents, what little they know—bless them.

Proud they weren’t when I told them I was taking my monumental achievement to the military. The Army accepted my voluntary application. I would even be paid to learn. I couldn’t understand the tears pouring down my mom’s cheeks. “Don’t let him go!” My dad was unresponsive. No sign of concern. Yet he spoke, in his way, with his intelligent brow, unsympathetic smirk, and unforgettable eyes—He needs us, let him learn that the hard way.

The ways of learning weren't divergent in the mind of my parents. Everything converged to a parent-tested path. You think you know how to sweep the floor, make a bed, wash the dishes, fold the laundry, or take out the trash. Trust me, you don’t. You don’t know the right way. The best way. The only way. And they will be the first to tell you that you don’t. Not a thank you for trying old sport. Do you not have any common sense?! The path always began and ended at common sense. Don’t you know how to check the oil, change a flat, or drive a manual transmission—it’s common sense. Was I born to do all those things?
